# The Founder Decision Log
## Using AI as a Thought Partner for Major Business Decisions

Most business decisions are made informally, under pressure, with incomplete information, and then immediately forgotten. You make the call, move on, and never revisit whether it was right or wrong. That means you learn slowly, you repeat mistakes, and you lose the institutional knowledge that lives only in your head.

The decision log fixes all three problems. It forces you to think clearly before you decide, creates a searchable record of your reasoning, and builds a feedback loop that makes you a better decision-maker over time.

---

## When to Log a Decision

Not every small judgment call needs to be logged. Log decisions that meet any of these criteria:

- The outcome will still matter in 30+ days
- You're spending more than 30 minutes debating it mentally
- It involves committing money, time, or a relationship
- It's a strategic direction that limits other options
- You might need to explain it to someone later (investor, partner, team member)

**Examples of decisions to log:**
- Pricing changes
- New product or feature launches
- Hiring a contractor or employee
- Choosing a vendor or platform
- Pivoting strategy or focus
- Partnership or collaboration agreements
- Any spend over your personal "significant amount" threshold

---

## How to Use AI as a Thought Partner Before Deciding

The single most powerful use of AI in decision-making is not to make the decision for you — it's to surface the questions you haven't asked yet.

Run this prompt before any major decision:

```
I'm making a decision: [describe it in one clear sentence]

Full context:
- Background: [why is this decision on the table?]
- What I know for certain: [facts]
- What I'm assuming: [beliefs I'm acting as if they're facts]
- What I don't know: [information gaps]
- Time pressure: [does this need to be decided now, or is there room to gather more data?]

My current leaning: [which option am I leaning toward and why?]

Challenge my reasoning:
1. What's the strongest argument against my current leaning?
2. What assumptions am I making that might not be true?
3. What information would change my decision, and can I get it before deciding?
4. What am I probably not seeing because I'm too close to this?
5. What's the real cost of deciding wrong? What's the real cost of delaying?
6. What would a founder who has made this mistake before tell me?
```

Take the AI's response seriously. It won't always be right, but it will reliably surface blindspots — and that alone is worth the 5 minutes.

## Building the Habit

The decision log only delivers value if you review it. Set a recurring 30-minute slot each quarter to read through your entries and look for patterns.

Run this quarterly review prompt:

```
Here are my last [X] decision log entries: [paste entries]

Review them and tell me:
1. What patterns do you see in the types of decisions I struggle with most?
2. Are there recurring situations where my initial instinct was wrong?
3. Am I making any of the classic decision-making biases repeatedly? (sunk cost, confirmation bias, overconfidence, etc.)
4. What one change to how I make decisions would have improved the most outcomes?
5. What's the decision I probably should have made sooner than I did?
```

The founders who compound their decision-making quality over time don't just make good decisions — they learn from the ones that didn't work. The decision log is how you turn experience into wisdom.

## Sample Entry: A Pricing Decision

**Entry #:** 7  
**Date:** January 15  
**Category:** Sales/Revenue  

**The Decision:** Whether to raise my consulting rate from $150/hour to $225/hour.

**Context:** I've been at $150/hour for 18 months. I'm fully booked and turning away clients. I'm undercharging by market standards based on my research. But I'm nervous about losing current clients.

**Final Decision:** Raise to $200/hour for new clients immediately. Grandfather current clients at $165 for 6 months, then $200.

**Reasoning:** The AI analysis helped me see that my real fear was rejection, not business logic. Market rates support $225. I split the difference to manage risk. The "grandfathering" approach protects current relationships.

**Expected Outcome (90 days):** 2–3 current clients may leave. New clients will close at the higher rate without resistance. Net revenue should be equal or higher.

**Review Date:** April 15

*(Review filled in April 15:)* **What happened:** Lost 1 client (expected). Closed 4 new clients at $200 without pushback. One client asked for $175 and I held the line — they agreed. Monthly revenue up 28%. Should have done this 12 months ago.

**Rating:** Great call. Delayed too long.
