# 25 Copy-Paste Prompts for Solo Founders
## Ready-to-Use AI Prompts Organized by Business Function

These are not generic prompts. Each one is built for the specific situation a solo founder faces, with enough context structure to get expert-level output on the first try. Copy, fill in the brackets, and use immediately.

---

## SECTION 1: Content (5 Prompts)

### Prompt 1 — Long-Form Blog Post Generator

**When to use:** You need a full-length, SEO-ready article on a topic in your industry.

**Context:** Works best when you have a keyword target and a clear audience in mind.

```
You are a content strategist and expert writer in [your industry].
Write a comprehensive 1,200-word blog post titled: "[your title]"

Target audience: [describe your ideal reader — their role, pain points, level of sophistication]
Target keyword: [primary keyword]
Tone: [direct and practical / warm and approachable / authoritative]

Structure:
- Hook opening (2–3 sentences that grab attention with a surprising insight or bold claim)
- 4 main sections with H2 subheadings, each 200–250 words
- Each section should include a concrete example or actionable takeaway
- Closing paragraph with a clear CTA: [describe desired action]

Do not: Use generic filler phrases, start sentences with "In conclusion," or use more than 2 exclamation points in the entire piece.
```

**Expected output:** A complete, structured blog post ready for light editing and publishing.

### Prompt 2 — Social Media Content Repurposer

**When to use:** You've written a blog post, sent an email, or done an interview — and you need to extract social content from it.

```
Here is a piece of content I created: [paste the content]

Repurpose it into 5 social media formats:
1. LinkedIn post (150 words, insight-driven, first-person, 3–4 paragraphs)
2. Twitter/X thread (8 tweets — hook, 6 body tweets, 1 CTA tweet)
3. Instagram caption (100 words, conversational, ends with a question)
4. Short-form video script (45 seconds — hook → insight → takeaway → CTA)
5. Email teaser (50 words, designed to drive click-through to the original piece)

Keep my voice: [describe your tone — e.g., direct and no-nonsense / energetic and motivational]
Core message to preserve across all formats: [one sentence]
```

**Expected output:** Five pieces of content from one source. A full week of social media from one session.

### Prompt 3 — Email Newsletter Writer

**When to use:** You need to write your weekly or monthly email newsletter but you're staring at a blank page.

```
Write a 400-word email newsletter for my audience of [describe audience].
My business: [describe what you do]
This week's theme/topic: [topic]
One key insight or story I want to share: [paste your raw idea in 3–5 sentences]

Format:
- Subject line (write 3 options — one curiosity-driven, one benefit-driven, one direct)
- Opening hook (2 sentences — reference the theme in a way that feels personal)
- Main body: one key insight, illustrated with a specific example
- Practical takeaway: one action the reader can take today
- Closing: warm sign-off + 1 sentence teasing next week's topic

Tone: [your tone]. This email should feel like a letter from a smart friend, not a corporate newsletter.
```

**Expected output:** A complete email newsletter with subject line variants, ready to send in Mailchimp, ConvertKit, or Beehiiv.

### Prompt 4 — Product Description Writer

**When to use:** You're listing a product or service for sale and need copy that converts.

```
Write a product description for: [product/service name]
What it is: [describe it in plain terms]
Who it's for: [target customer — be specific]
The #1 problem it solves: [the real pain point]
Key features or deliverables: [list 3–5 bullet points]
Price: [price point]
Guarantee or risk-reversal: [e.g., 30-day refund, results guarantee]

Write:
1. A short version (50 words) — for product cards, ads, or social bios
2. A medium version (150 words) — for checkout pages or email
3. A long version (300 words) — for a full landing page section

Use the Problem → Agitate → Solution → CTA structure in the long version.
Avoid: buzzwords, vague claims, anything that can't be verified.
```

**Expected output:** Three ready-to-use descriptions at different lengths for different placements.

### Prompt 5 — Content Calendar Generator

**When to use:** You need to plan a full month of content and don't want to think about it from scratch.

```
I run [describe business]. My audience is [describe].
I want to create consistent content across: [LinkedIn / Newsletter / Instagram / Twitter / YouTube — pick your channels]
My content pillars are: [list 3–4 themes you regularly talk about]
This month's business goal: [e.g., launch a new product, grow email list, drive leads]

Create a 4-week content calendar with:
- 3 pieces of content per week (one for each channel or one per format)
- A topic for each piece tied to my pillars and business goal
- One "pillar" post per week (longer, high-value content that anchors the rest)
- 2 "supporting" posts per week (shorter, conversational, drives engagement)

Format as a table: Week | Day | Channel | Topic | Format | Goal
```

**Expected output:** A full 4-week content calendar, ready to execute.

## SECTION 2: Sales (5 Prompts)

### Prompt 6 — Cold Outreach Email Writer

**When to use:** You need to reach out to a prospect you've never spoken to.

```
You are a B2B sales expert who specializes in [your industry].
Write a cold outreach email to: [prospect name and title] at [company name]

About them (paste what you know): [LinkedIn summary, company about page, recent news, etc.]
My product/service: [1-sentence description]
The specific problem my product solves for someone in their role: [be precise]
Desired CTA: [schedule a 15-min call / reply yes or no / check out this resource]

Rules:
- Under 100 words
- First line must reference something specific about them
- No "I hope this finds you well"
- No buzzwords
- Sound like a real person who did 5 minutes of research, not a mass-email robot
```

**Expected output:** A concise, personalized cold email that doesn't look like every other cold email.

### Prompt 7 — Objection Handler

**When to use:** A prospect raised a specific objection and you need to respond without being defensive or pushy.

```
I'm in a sales conversation and received this objection: "[paste the exact objection]"

Context: [brief description of the prospect, what they do, and what we discussed]
My product/service: [description]
The real value it delivers: [what outcome does the customer get?]

Write 3 responses to this objection:
1. A direct, confident response that reframes the objection as a sign of fit
2. A question that uncovers whether this objection is real or a smokescreen
3. A story-based response: how a similar customer had the same concern and what happened after they moved forward

Each response should be under 75 words. Tone: confident but not aggressive.
```

**Expected output:** Three objection responses you can test in real sales conversations.

### Prompt 8 — Proposal Writer

**When to use:** A prospect is ready for a proposal and you need to write one that wins.

```
Write a business proposal for the following engagement:
Client: [company name, industry, size]
Their stated problem: [what they told you in discovery]
Underlying problem (what you believe is really going on): [your assessment]
Proposed solution: [describe what you'll do, in plain language]
Deliverables: [specific outputs]
Timeline: [start date, milestones, end date]
Investment: [pricing — present it confidently, not apologetically]
Why us: [your key differentiator for this specific client]

Format:
- Executive summary (2 paragraphs — problem and solution)
- Scope of work (bulleted list of deliverables)
- Timeline (table format)
- Investment (clean pricing section with optional upsell)
- Why now (1 paragraph — the cost of inaction)
- Next steps (clear, specific, with a deadline)
```

**Expected output:** A complete, professional proposal structure you can drop into your own template.

### Prompt 9 — Follow-Up Sequence Builder

**When to use:** You've had a discovery call or sent a proposal and the prospect has gone quiet.

```
A prospect [describe: role, company type, what was discussed] has gone quiet after [number of days].
Last communication: [summarize the last exchange in 2 sentences]
What's at stake: [deal size or strategic importance]

Write a 3-touch follow-up sequence:
- Touch 1 (Day 3 after silence): Soft check-in, add one piece of value (tip, relevant article, insight)
- Touch 2 (Day 7): Reference the problem they mentioned in discovery; offer a new angle
- Touch 3 (Day 14): The breakup email — honest, no desperation, leaves the door open

Each email: under 80 words. The goal is to re-engage, not to pressure.
```

**Expected output:** A three-email follow-up sequence that feels human and earns responses.

### Prompt 10 — Testimonial Request and Case Study Builder

**When to use:** You've delivered results for a customer and want to capture that story for social proof.

```
I delivered [describe outcome] for a customer: [brief description of who they are]
Before working with me, their situation was: [describe the problem they had]
What I did: [describe the work or product they used]
The result: [specific, measurable outcome if possible]

Do two things:
1. Write a testimonial request email that asks for 3–4 sentences covering: what their situation was before, what changed, and what they'd tell someone considering working with me. Make it easy to say yes with guiding questions.

2. Write a short-form case study (250 words): Client Profile → Problem → Solution → Result → Quote placeholder. Format it for a sales page or proposal.
```

**Expected output:** A testimonial request email and a ready-to-publish case study.

## SECTION 3: Research (5 Prompts)

### Prompt 11 — Competitor Analysis Generator

```
Research my top 5 competitors in [industry/niche].
My product/service: [describe it]
My target market: [describe your customers]

For each competitor, provide:
- Company name and one-sentence description
- Pricing model (if available)
- Core value proposition
- Who they serve best
- Key weaknesses (look for G2, Trustpilot, Reddit, and App Store reviews)
- Feature gap: what they don't offer that my customers might want

At the end, give me a 3-sentence positioning recommendation: where is the white space I can own?
```

### Prompt 12 — Customer Feedback Synthesizer

```
Here is raw customer feedback I've collected: [paste reviews, survey responses, or support tickets]

Synthesize this into:
1. Top 3 recurring pain points (quote frequency: how many times each was mentioned)
2. Top 3 things customers love (same format)
3. The one thing customers want most that I'm not currently delivering
4. Suggested product improvement or feature based on this data
5. Two pull quotes I could use in my marketing (pick the most vivid and specific feedback)
```

### Prompt 13 — Market Research Briefing

```
I'm exploring [market/industry segment] as a potential opportunity.
My current business: [describe]
The opportunity I'm considering: [describe the new product or market]

Research this for me and provide:
1. Market size estimate (TAM/SAM/SOM if possible)
2. Key trends driving growth or decline in this space
3. Top 3–5 established players and their positioning
4. Underserved customer segment I might be able to target
5. The biggest risk in entering this market
6. Your honest assessment: is this opportunity attractive for a solo founder? Why or why not?
```

### Prompt 14 — Trend Analysis and Opportunity Spotter

```
I'm in [your industry]. Analyze the following trends I've been noticing: [list 3–5 things you've observed]

For each trend:
1. Is this a real macro trend or a temporary spike? What evidence supports each case?
2. Who is currently benefiting from this trend?
3. What product or service could a solo founder build in the next 90 days to capitalize on it?
4. What would be the fastest way to validate the opportunity before investing significant time?

Conclude with your top recommendation: which of these trends has the most viable opportunity for a small operator?
```

### Prompt 15 — Survey Design and Analysis Helper

```
I want to survey my target customers about [topic or product concept].
My hypothesis: [what you believe to be true]
Target respondent: [describe who you want to survey]

Design a 10-question survey that:
- Starts with screening questions to ensure respondent fit
- Uses a mix of multiple choice, Likert scale (1–5 agreement), and open-ended questions
- Avoids leading questions
- Includes one question that helps me understand willingness to pay
- Ends with an open invitation for a follow-up conversation

After I collect responses, I'll paste the data back and ask you to synthesize the findings.
```

## SECTION 4: Operations (5 Prompts)

### Prompt 16 — SOP Writer

```
I need to document a Standard Operating Procedure for: [task name]
Here's how I currently do it: [describe the process step by step, even informally]

Write a formal SOP that includes:
- Purpose: why this task matters to the business
- Who is responsible: [role that will execute this]
- Tools required: [software, platforms, or equipment needed]
- Step-by-step procedure (numbered, written for someone doing this for the first time)
- Quality check: how do we know this was done correctly?
- What to do if something goes wrong: 2–3 common failure points and fixes
- Time estimate: how long should this take?
```

### Prompt 17 — Vendor Evaluation Framework

```
I'm evaluating vendors for [service/tool category].
My requirements:
- Must have: [list non-negotiables]
- Nice to have: [list preferences]
- Budget range: [range per month or year]
- My business context: [brief description]

Here are the vendors I'm considering: [list them with any relevant notes]

Create a comparison framework:
1. Score each vendor on: price/value, feature fit, ease of use, support quality, scalability
2. Flag any deal-breakers based on my must-haves
3. Give me your top recommendation and why
4. List 3 questions I should ask each vendor before deciding
```

### Prompt 19 — Process Efficiency Audit

```
I'm spending too much time on [specific process or area of the business].
Here's what I do each week in this area: [describe the tasks, time estimates per task]
Here are the tools I currently use: [list them]

Audit this and tell me:
```

## SECTION 5: Strategy (5 Prompts)

### Prompt 21 — Business Model Stress Test

```
Here is my current business model: [describe how you make money, pricing, customer acquisition, delivery]
Revenue last 3 months: [amount]
Main growth drivers: [what's working]
Main constraints: [what's limiting growth]

Stress test this model:
1. What breaks first if I try to 2x revenue?
2. What is my biggest single point of failure right now?
3. If my main acquisition channel disappeared tomorrow, what would I do?
4. What assumptions am I making about my customers that might not hold at larger scale?
5. What does this model look like in 2 years if everything goes right? If everything goes wrong?
```

### Prompt 22 — Annual Planning Framework

```
I'm planning my next 12 months. Here's where I stand today:
- Current monthly revenue: [amount]
- Main product/service: [describe]
- Team: [just me / me + contractors]
- What's working: [list]
- What's not: [list]
- My 12-month revenue goal: [amount]

Build a 4-quarter plan:
- Q1 Goal and 3 key initiatives
- Q2 Goal and 3 key initiatives
- Q3 Goal and 3 key initiatives
- Q4 Goal and 3 key initiatives

Then tell me: What's the single riskiest assumption in this plan? And what's the most likely reason I'll miss the annual goal?
```

### Prompt 23 — Pivot Evaluation Framework

```
I'm considering pivoting from [current product/model] to [new direction].
Reasons I'm considering this pivot:
- [reason 1]
- [reason 2]
- [reason 3]

Current business data: [revenue, customers, traction metrics]

Evaluate this pivot:
1. Am I pivoting because of real evidence or because I'm bored/frustrated?
2. Does the new direction solve a validated problem with real demand?
3. What do I keep from my current business (skills, customers, brand)?
4. What's the minimum experiment I could run to test the pivot hypothesis before committing?
5. If I were an investor, would I fund this pivot? Why or why not?
```

### Prompt 24 — Pricing Strategy Optimizer

```
I'm evaluating my pricing. Current situation:
- Product/service: [describe]
- Current price: [amount]
- Customer profile: [describe who buys and why]
- Close rate: [% of leads who convert]
- Main objection to price: [what prospects say]

Analyze and recommend:
1. Is my current price too low, about right, or potentially too high relative to the value delivered?
2. What pricing model fits my product best: one-time, subscription, usage-based, tiered, or value-based?
3. Design a 3-tier pricing structure (Starter / Pro / Premium) with prices and feature differentiation
4. What one thing could I add to my offering to justify charging 30–50% more?
5. Script for how to present the price in a sales conversation without apologizing for it
```

### Prompt 25 — Strategic Priority-Setting for a Crossroads Decision

```
I'm at a crossroads in my business and need to make a strategic decision.

Here's the full context: [explain your situation in as much detail as you can — 3–5 paragraphs]

The options I'm weighing:
Option A: [describe] — potential upside: [what could go right] / risk: [what could go wrong]
Option B: [describe] — potential upside: / risk:
Option C: [describe — can be "do nothing" or "wait"]

I want you to:
1. Identify any options I haven't considered
2. Tell me what information I'm missing that would change the decision
3. Give me your honest recommendation — not a diplomatic "it depends" — with your reasoning
4. Tell me what the 50-year-old version of me, looking back, would think of each choice
```

## How to Build Your Own Prompt Library

Copy any of these prompts into a personal library (Notion, Google Doc, or Apple Notes). After you use a prompt, add a note: *"This worked well for X, modify Y next time."* Your prompts will get sharper with every use.

The real leverage isn't in any single prompt — it's in building a personal library of tested, refined prompts that fit your voice, your industry, and your specific business context. Every hour you invest in your prompt library pays back for years.
